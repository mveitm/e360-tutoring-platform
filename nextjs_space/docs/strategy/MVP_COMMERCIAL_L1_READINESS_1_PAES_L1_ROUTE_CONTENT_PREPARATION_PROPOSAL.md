## 5. Executive summary

The external proposal argues that L1 cannot be treated as a generic reading-comprehension quiz.

A credible Competencia Lectora L1 line would need to demonstrate that Bexauri can guide reading, not merely count answers. It should have:

* a distinct reading-competence identity;
* candidate route and text families;
* L1-specific StudyLoad families;
* feedback adapted to textual evidence, inference, purpose, structure, contextual vocabulary, distractors, and justification of alternatives;
* dashboard/progress evidence separated from M1 and M2;
* later official-source alignment;
* later text rights/licensing review;
* later pedagogical and editorial review;
* later content/text validation;
* later app/student evidence.

This phase versions the proposal only. It creates no L1 implementation.

## 6. Governing principle for L1

Candidate principle:

L1 should guide the student to build comprehension from textual evidence, identifying explicit information, relationships between ideas, reasonable inferences, communicative purpose, and contextual meaning.

Safe interpretation:

* L1 should be a distinct tutoria, not a generic quiz.
* L1 should teach reading strategy, not just reveal correct answers.
* L1 should require later official-source alignment before any readiness claim.
* L1 should require later text rights/licensing review before any text is used.
* L1 should require later pedagogical and editorial review before any sellable-offer claim.
* L1 should require later content validation and app/student evidence before any public Sales-Ready claim.

## 7. L1 vs M1/M2 distinction

Candidate distinction:

* Math tutorias work with procedures, models, formal representations, symbolic reasoning, and calculation.
* L1 works with comprehension, interpretation, evidence, language, context, and critical reading.

L1 should not be:

* M1/M2 interaction patterns with text pasted into them;
* a generic multiple-choice quiz;
* an answer-key experience;
* a dashboard card without reading route/content identity;
* texts and questions the system cannot justify with textual evidence;
* official PAES L1 alignment unless a later source-alignment phase supports that claim.

## 8. Candidate L1 identity

Candidate L1 identity may include:

* short or medium texts with controlled cognitive load;
* questions tied to explicit reading skills;
* feedback that cites or points to textual evidence without overloading the student;
* explanation of why the correct alternative is supported;
* explanation of why distractors are incorrect;
* progress separated by reading skill;
* distinction between literal, inferential, and interpretive comprehension;
* editorial care around relevance, rights, difficulty, clarity, and ambiguity.

This identity requires later official PAES L1 source/blueprint alignment and editorial validation.

## 9. Candidate L1 route map

Candidate route map:

1. Initial reading diagnostic.
2. Locate evidence.
3. Controlled inference.
4. Purpose and viewpoint.
5. Contextual vocabulary.
6. Relationship between ideas and structure.
7. Integrated questions.

Safe adjustment:

These are candidate route families only. They are not approved official L1 blocks, not final taxonomy, and not implementation scope.

## 10. Candidate text types

Candidate text types may include:

* short expository texts;
* short argumentative texts;
* adapted or licensed columns/comments only if rights and difficulty are controlled;
* divulgative texts;
* short narrative fragments if cognitive load is controlled;
* simple discontinuous texts such as tables, posters, or textual infographics if scope is defined.

No text type is approved for use by this phase.

Every future text family would require source, rights/licensing, difficulty, bias/sensitivity, editorial quality, and PAES relevance review before implementation or public-readiness use.

## 12. L1 feedback expectations

Aligned with Gate 1B, L1 feedback would need its own reading-specific identity.

Candidate feedback breve should indicate:

* answer state;
* reading skill involved;
* textual or strategic hint;
* what to review, such as fragment, connector, purpose, keyword, relationship between ideas, or distractor alternative.

Candidate feedback completo should include:

* correct answer;
* relevant textual evidence without excessive copying;
* explanation of the reading skill;
* reasoning that connects the question, text, and correct alternative;
* why incorrect alternatives fail;
* likely reading error, such as partial reading, excessive inference, confusion between data and purpose, or vocabulary interpreted outside context.

L1 feedback should teach reading strategy, not just reveal the answer.

## 13. L1 dashboard/progress expectations

Aligned with Gate 1C, L1 dashboard/progress evidence would need to show L1 separately from M1 and M2.

Candidate L1 dashboard/progress evidence may include:

* L1 enrollment state;
* current or next L1 StudyLoad;
* completed L1 loads;
* reading skills practiced;
* text types practiced;
* latest L1 StudyLoads;
* strengths and weaknesses by reading skill when supported by actual L1 evidence;
* recurring reading errors;
* difficulty or text-load evolution;
* feedback availability for L1 work;
* progress in reading comprehension, not only count of answers.

Dashboard evidence must not imply L1 readiness without L1 route/content, text, question, feedback, and app/student evidence.

## 14. Minimum evidence that L1 is not nominal

Candidate minimum evidence that L1 is not nominal:

* L1 taxonomy separate from M1 and M2.
* Texts selected or produced with editorial criteria.
* StudyLoads associated with real reading skills.
* Questions that require textual evidence, not superficial memory.
* Feedback breve and feedback completo specific to reading.
* Distractor explanation.
* Dashboard/progress evidence with differentiated reading progress.
* Pedagogical review of difficulty, relevance, and cognitive load.
* Student validation before considering L1 part of a sellable offer.

This evidence does not exist because of this document. It would require future content, text, implementation, rights, review, and validation phases.

## 15. Risks of implementing L1 as generic quiz

| Risk | Why it matters | Mitigation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Experience feels like a question bank without tutoring | Weakens the learning promise and student trust. | Require guided reading identity and feedback that teaches strategy. |
| Feedback is poor or generic | Student may see answers without learning how to read better. | Require reading-specific brief and complete feedback. |
| Texts are too long, biased, irrelevant, or rights-problematic | Creates comprehension, legal, ethical, and brand risk. | Require editorial, rights/licensing, and cognitive-load review. |
| Questions are ambiguous | Damages trust and makes feedback hard to justify. | Require clear correction criteria and textual evidence. |
| Distractors are weak or unfair | Student cannot learn from alternatives. | Require distractor review and explanation where useful. |
| Dashboard measures activity but not reading progress | Inflates readiness without L1 evidence. | Separate L1 dashboard/progress by reading skill and text work. |
| Literal comprehension and interpretation are confused | Feedback may reward shallow or unsupported reading. | Distinguish literal, inferential, and interpretive tasks. |
| System justifies answers badly | High trust damage in language/reading domains. | Require robust textual justification and editorial review. |
| L1 coverage is declared without evidence | Weakens the public M1/M2/L1 Sales-Ready gate. | Keep L1 not ready until route/content and student evidence exist. |

## 16. L1 NO-GO boundaries

L1 remains `NO-GO` for readiness if:

* texts are long without cognitive-load control;
* texts have uncertain rights;
* sensitive or controversial texts lack review;
* questions are ambiguous;
* questions depend on external knowledge instead of textual evidence;
* generic feedback is reused from M1 or M2;
* complete L1 simulations are promised without validated coverage;
* advanced skills are introduced without robust correction criteria;
* complex literary texts are used without solid interpretive feedback capacity;
* questions cannot be justified with textual evidence;
* official PAES L1 alignment is claimed without later official-source review;
* public copy promises complete L1 preparation.
