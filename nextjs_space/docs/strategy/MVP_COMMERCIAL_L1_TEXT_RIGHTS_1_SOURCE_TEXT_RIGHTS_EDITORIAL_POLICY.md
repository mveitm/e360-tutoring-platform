# MVP-COMMERCIAL-L1-TEXT-RIGHTS-1 - Source/Text Rights and Editorial Policy

## 1. Phase

* Phase: `MVP-COMMERCIAL-L1-TEXT-RIGHTS-1`.
* Type: documentation-only / L1 text-rights policy / editorial-quality boundary.
* Product horizon: roadmap change-control / L1 text-rights and editorial policy / Sales-Ready support.
* This is not `MVP-Beta-Sales-Ready` approval.
* M2 remains not ready.
* L1 remains not ready.
* Public Sales-Ready remains blocked.

## 2. Relationship to L1-READINESS-1 and L1-ALIGNMENT-1

`MVP-COMMERCIAL-L1-READINESS-1` versioned a non-binding candidate L1 route/content preparation proposal and identified text rights, licensing, editorial quality, ambiguity, cognitive load, and feedback as future dependencies.

`MVP-COMMERCIAL-PAES-L1-ALIGNMENT-1` mapped the candidate L1 route and StudyLoad families to SOURCE-1C high-level official L1 summaries and kept L1 taxonomy, texts, questions, StudyLoads, feedback, dashboard evidence, implementation, and readiness blocked.

This document defines a conservative policy boundary for future L1 text sourcing and editorial review before any L1 content phase. It does not select, create, approve, or implement texts.

## 3. Nature of document

This is a policy boundary, not legal advice.

It is:

* documentation-only;
* conservative;
* source-class oriented;
* editorial-quality oriented;
* intended to reduce future rights, ambiguity, and brand risk before L1 content work.

It is not:

* a legal opinion;
* text selection;
* text creation;
* question creation;
* StudyLoad design;
* content registry authority;
* feedback implementation;
* L1 readiness approval;
* Sales-Ready approval.

## 4. Purpose

The purpose is to define safe rules for future L1 text handling before any text, question, StudyLoad, registry, feedback, dashboard, or implementation work.

This phase defines:

* candidate-allowed, conditionally allowed, blocked, and reserved source/text classes;
* copyright and licensing boundaries;
* editorial-quality review criteria;
* cognitive-load and difficulty criteria;
* sensitivity, bias, controversy, and age/context criteria;
* ambiguity and answerability criteria;
* feedback/evidence citation boundaries;
* future metadata requirements for any actual text assets;
* PASS / FAIL / BLOCKED criteria for future content phases.

## 5. Policy principles

Future L1 text work should follow these principles:

* Prefer original Bexauri-created texts for early L1 pilot content because rights custody is clearest.
* Treat every non-original text as a rights and editorial-review problem before product use.
* Treat official PAES/DEMRE released texts and examples as alignment/reference sources by default, not product content.
* Do not copy official PAES/DEMRE text passages, item wording, or questions into Bexauri content without separate explicit authorization.
* Keep L1 text-bound. L1 is reading work tied to texts, not a generic quiz or standalone vocabulary drill.
* Require a single defensible correct answer for future multiple-choice questions.
* Require textual evidence availability for the answer and for plausible distractor review.
* Avoid excessive cognitive load in early content.
* Block sensitive or controversial text use by default until Pro/high-level/editorial review approves it.
* Keep legal-sensitive questions reserved for Pro/high-level/legal direction.

## 7. Original Bexauri-created text policy

Original Bexauri-created texts are the preferred candidate source class for early L1 pilot content.

They are preferred because Bexauri can control:

* rights custody;
* text length;
* vocabulary;
* cognitive load;
* ambiguity;
* cultural/contextual fit;
* question and feedback alignment;
* future revision.

Original text is still not automatically approved. Before use, each text must pass editorial review, difficulty control, bias/sensitivity review, ambiguity review, answer validation, and metadata capture.

## 8. Public-domain text policy

Public-domain texts are candidate-allowed only when public-domain status is verified and documented.

Future use requires:

* documented source and public-domain basis;
* jurisdiction/date review when relevant;
* attribution where appropriate;
* adaptation policy if the text is shortened, modernized, translated, or excerpted;
* editorial review for cognitive load, vocabulary, age/context fit, ambiguity, and answerability.

Public-domain status does not remove the need for L1 alignment or editorial quality.

## 9. Licensed text policy

Licensed texts are conditionally allowed only if the license terms are verified and compatible with the intended Bexauri use.

Future metadata must capture:

* licensor/source;
* license type;
* permitted uses;
* adaptation rights;
* attribution requirements;
* commercial-use compatibility;
* duration or revocation limits if any;
* storage and display constraints.

If license terms are unclear, the text is blocked until Pro/high-level/legal review.

## 10. Official/released PAES/DEMRE text policy

Official/released PAES/DEMRE texts, examples, questions, item wording, and answer materials are reference and alignment sources by default.

Product use is reserved and requires separate explicit rights/legal/editorial review.

Future phases must not:

* copy official PAES/DEMRE passages into product content without authorization;
* copy official PAES/DEMRE item wording into Bexauri questions without authorization;
* imply official endorsement;
* treat released/example items as free product content;
* use official texts as a shortcut around Bexauri editorial creation.

Official sources may inform high-level alignment, item-style awareness, and source-custody planning under copyright-safe limits.

## 11. Adapted text policy

Adapted texts are high-risk unless the source rights clearly allow adaptation or the source is original Bexauri-created/public-domain with documented status.

Adaptation includes:

* shortening;
* paraphrasing;
* translating;
* modernizing;
* changing names or context;
* combining parts of multiple texts;
* converting a continuous text into discontinuous or multimodal material.

Future metadata must record the original source, rights basis, adaptation type, adaptation purpose, editor, review status, and any attribution requirement.

## 12. Student/user-provided text policy

Student or user-provided texts are blocked for default product content.

They may be considered only in a future explicit phase that handles:

* privacy;
* consent;
* minors/guardian boundaries where relevant;
* moderation;
* harmful or sensitive content;
* ownership and reuse rights;
* deletion/retention;
* whether the text may be shown to anyone other than the submitter.

No student/user-provided text is approved by this phase.

## 13. Web/news/blog/social text policy

Web, news, blog, and social-media texts are blocked by default for product use.

They may be considered only if:

* rights are licensed or clearly permitted for intended use;
* commercial use and adaptation rights are compatible;
* attribution is documented;
* content is editorially appropriate;
* sensitivity, bias, and date/context drift are reviewed.

Screenshots, copied posts, paywalled articles, and scraped passages must not be treated as safe product content.

## 14. Sensitive/controversial text policy

Sensitive or controversial texts are blocked by default for early L1 content.

This includes texts centered on topics likely to create age/context, political, religious, trauma, discrimination, violence, sexuality, health, legal, or identity-related risk.

Future use requires Pro/high-level/editorial approval and must define:

* pedagogical purpose;
* age/context fit;
* bias and harm review;
* support/escalation boundary;
* answerability and ambiguity controls;
* whether the topic is necessary for L1 goals.

## 15. Literary fragment policy

Literary fragments are conditional.

They require:

* rights verification;
* excerpt boundary review;
* interpretive ambiguity review;
* age/context appropriateness;
* clear evidence for any answer;
* careful distractor design;
* feedback that avoids pretending only one broad literary interpretation exists unless the question is tightly constrained.

Literary difficulty must be controlled before early L1 use.

## 16. Discontinuous/multimodal text policy

Discontinuous or multimodal text-like materials are conditional.

Examples may include tables, posters, forms, simple infographics, diagrams with text, or mixed-format materials.

Future use requires:

* rights-safe source or original creation;
* accessibility review;
* legibility review across target devices;
* cognitive-load review;
* clear text-referenced question design;
* alignment to L1 reading tasks rather than visual puzzle behavior.

## 17. Editorial-quality criteria

Future L1 texts and questions should be reviewed for:

* clear reading purpose;
* appropriate length;
* appropriate vocabulary;
* age/context appropriateness;
* cognitive load;
* ambiguity;
* single defensible correct answer for multiple-choice;
* quality of distractors;
* textual evidence availability;
* no requirement for external knowledge unless explicitly part of the task;
* bias/sensitivity;
* cultural context;
* accessibility;
* feedback feasibility.

Editorial review must happen before any student-facing use.

## 18. Cognitive-load and difficulty criteria

Future L1 text difficulty should be controlled through:

* text length;
* sentence complexity;
* vocabulary density;
* inferential burden;
* number of ideas and relationships;
* layout and visual complexity;
* familiarity of context;
* number and similarity of distractors;
* amount of evidence the student must hold in memory.

Early L1 content should prefer short, focused texts and questions that isolate a reading skill without reducing L1 to superficial scanning.

## 19. Ambiguity and answerability criteria

Future L1 multiple-choice questions must have one defensible correct answer.

Before use, reviewers should confirm:

* the answer is supported by the text;
* distractors are plausible but wrong for identifiable textual or reasoning reasons;
* no external knowledge is required unless the task explicitly defines it;
* wording does not create accidental multiple answers;
* feedback can explain the answer without overquoting copyrighted text;
* ambiguous literary or opinion-based readings are not forced into false certainty.

If answerability cannot be defended, the item is blocked.

## 20. Feedback/evidence citation boundary

Future feedback may point to or briefly paraphrase relevant evidence.

Future feedback should avoid reproducing long copyrighted passages, official texts, or externally licensed text chunks unless a rights policy explicitly allows it.

Feedback design must define:

* how to identify the relevant section of a text;
* when short paraphrase is enough;
* when a brief quote is allowed by rights policy;
* how to explain distractors without copying too much text;
* how to keep feedback readable and text-evidence aware.

This phase does not create feedback.

## 21. Storage and metadata requirements for future text assets

Future text assets should not be created until a separate content phase defines and captures metadata.

Required future metadata should include:

* text ID;
* source class;
* source title or internal title;
* author/creator where relevant;
* rights basis;
* license or public-domain evidence where relevant;
* attribution requirement;
* adaptation status;
* editor/reviewer;
* reading purpose;
* text type;
* difficulty estimate;
* length;
* sensitivity flags;
* ambiguity notes;
* accessibility notes;
* question linkage;
* feedback evidence boundary;
* approval status;
* version.

No content registry entries are created by this document.

## 22. PASS / FAIL / BLOCKED criteria

Documentation PASS for this phase means:

* source/text classes are classified conservatively;
* rights, licensing, official-source, adaptation, privacy, sensitivity, and editorial boundaries are documented;
* feedback/evidence citation boundaries are documented;
* future metadata needs are documented;
* all implementation, content, readiness, and Sales-Ready boundaries remain intact.

This phase would FAIL if it selects texts, creates texts, creates questions, creates StudyLoads, approves official PAES/DEMRE text use, approves copyrighted text use, implements L1, declares L1 ready, or approves Sales-Ready.

Future L1 content remains BLOCKED until a separate phase verifies text rights, editorial quality, question answerability, feedback feasibility, metadata, and governance approval.

## 23. Risks and mitigations

| Risk | Why it matters | Mitigation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Policy is mistaken for legal advice | Could create false rights confidence | State policy boundary and reserve legal-sensitive issues |
| Official PAES/DEMRE materials are copied into product | Creates rights and trust risk | Treat official materials as reference-only by default |
| Original texts are treated as automatically ready | Could create poor L1 quality | Require editorial, difficulty, ambiguity, and answer validation |
| Public-domain status is assumed | Rights may be wrong by jurisdiction/source | Require documented public-domain verification |
| Adaptations hide source rights issues | Paraphrase or shortening may still be restricted | Require source and adaptation metadata |
| L1 becomes generic quiz content | Breaks L1 identity | Require text-bound reading purpose and evidence |
| Feedback copies too much text | Creates rights and UX risk | Require evidence-reference boundary before feedback design |
| Sensitive texts create harm or support burden | Brand and student-safety risk | Block by default until high-level/editorial approval |

## 24. Questions reserved for Pro/high-level/legal/editorial direction

Reserved questions:

* Whether any official PAES/DEMRE text or item may ever be used in product.
* Who performs legal review for non-original text sources.
* Whether Bexauri should use only original texts for early L1.
* Which licenses are compatible with commercial tutoring use.
* How to handle minor/student-provided text submissions if ever considered.
* What sensitivity categories are allowed for early L1, if any.
* Minimum editorial review roles and signoff threshold.
* Whether literary fragments should be delayed until after simpler non-literary texts.
* Whether discontinuous/multimodal texts belong in initial L1 or later phases.
* How much text evidence feedback may quote or paraphrase.
